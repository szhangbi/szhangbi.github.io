Existence and lattice structure of stable matchings can be proven by Tarski’s fixed point theorem.

Posted on 2021-12-20 In Summary

This post serves as a summary of the paper On a characterization of stable matchings by Hiroyuki Adachi (2000).

Tarski's fixed point theorem states that, For\((L,\leq)\) being a complete lattice and \(f:L \to L\) being a monotone function w.r.t. \(\leq\), the set of fixed points of \(f\) forms a complete lattice.

The key of the proof is to identify a complete lattice structure where stable matchings can be considered as fixed points of some increasing function. And it turned out, we can consider pre-matchings to be the set \(L\) and common preference of man to be the partial order.

Informally, pre-matching is a looser concept than matching. A matching \(\mu: M \cup W \to M \cup W\) is a one-to-one correspondence and is onto itself of order 2(\(\mu(\mu(x))=x\)) as we require a man to be matched to the woman who is matched to him. Pre-matching only requires one-to-one, mathematically, a pre-matchinng can be described by a pair of function \(\nu \equiv (\nu_M,\nu_W)\) where \(\nu_M : M \to M \cup W\) s.t. if \(\nu_M(m) \notin W\) then \(\nu_M(m)=m\), and \(\nu_W : W \to M \cup W\) s.t. if \(\nu_W(w) \notin M\) then \(\nu_W(w)=w\). The set of all such pair of functions is called \(pre-matchings\), i.e. \(V:=V_M \times V_W\).

Common preference of man over pre-matching is a partial order such that a prematching is prefered to the other if every man prefers his pre-matched partner to that in the other pre-matching, mathematically,
\(\geq_M\) on \(V_M\) specifies \(\nu_M \geq_M \nu_M'\) if.f. \(\nu_M(m) \geq_M \nu_M'(m)\) for all m;
\(\geq_W\) on \(V_W\) specifies \(\nu_W \geq_W \nu_W'\) if.f. \(\nu_W(w) \geq_W \nu_W'(w)\) for all m;
\(\geqslant_M\) on \(V\) specifies \(\nu_M \geqslant_M \nu_M'\) if.f. \(\nu_M \geq_M \nu_M'\) and \(\nu_M \leq_W \nu_M'\).

\((V,\geqslant_M)\) is a complete lattice because it is finite.

All stable matchings can be considered as pre-matching that solves the following two equations
\(\nu_M(m)= \underset{w.r.t. \geq_m}{max}\{\{w \in W| m\geq_w \nu_W(w)\} \cup \{m\}\}\) for all \(m\in M\) (1)
\(\nu_W(w)= \underset{w.r.t. \geq_w}{max}\{\{m \in M| m\geq_m \nu_M(m)\} \cup \{w\}\}\) for all \(w\in W\) (2)
For proofs, one can refer to Prop. 3.4 of the original paper.

Consider a mapping \(T\equiv(T_1,T_2)\) with \(T_1: V \to V_M\), such that \(T_1(\nu)\) is the solution of (1), while \(T_2(\nu)\) is the solution of (2). It is not hard to show that \(T\) is non-decreasing in \(\nu\) w.r.t. \(\geqslant_M\).

A Stable matching \(\mu\) is a fixed point of \(T\) as it solves both equation (1,2) simultaneously. So, according to Tarski's fixed point theorem, the set of stable matchings (\(\hat{V}\) that solves (1,2)) is non-empty and \((\hat{V},\geqslant_M)\) is a complete lattice.

8 Things on EADAM (2)

Posted on 2021-09-01 Edited on 2021-09-16 In Summary

  1. One perspective provided by Tang & Yu (2014)

Focusing on each student's incentive to give consent or not in the school choice with consent model we discussed last time, Tang & Yu provided a new prospective of EADAM. It is reasoned that in order not to disincentivize a student to give consent, we should violate his priorities to allow improvement of others' assignment only when the assignment of this student can not be improved any more, so that his chance of getting into a better school would not be hurted by his consent. To identify which students are not Pareto-improvable given the outcome of DA, we could first identify which schools are under-demanded.

2.1 Definition of Under-demanded school: A school s is under-demanded at a matching \(\mu\) if no student prefers it to his assignment under \(\mu\), that is, \(\nexists i\), such that \(s \succ_{i} \mu(i)\).

The following lemma tells us that students who are matched with under-demanded school in a DA matching are not Pareto-improvable.

2.2 Lemma: At a DA matching, all students who are matched with under-demanded schools are not Pareto-improvable. Proof: Actually, this result also hold for any non-wasteful matching mechanisms, and this is because Pareto-improvements can only take the form of trading when the matching is non-wasteful. The intuition behind is straightforward, that is, no student would be willing to trade his assignment for a under-demanded school. For technical details, please refer to the Appendix A.1 of Tang & Yu's paper.

2.3 The simplified Efficiency-adjusted deferred acceptance mechanism (simplified EADAM):

Round 0: Run the DA algorithm.

Round k (\(k \geq 1\)): 1. Identify all the under-demanded schools at the (k-1)-th round, settle these schools as the final assignment for students who are matched with these schools, remove these schools and the matched students; 2. For each removed student i, check whether he gives consent or not. If not, for each school s that is preferred to his matching \(\mu(i)\), we modify the priority of s such that any student j who has a lower priority than i is considered as unacceptable for s1; 3. Stop if all schools are removed. Otherwise, re-run DA with the remaining students and schools (with their modified priorities).

2.4 Observation: The algorithm terminates after at most (|S|+1) rounds.

Proof: This is ensured by the existence of under-demanded school at the end of each round, which is guaranteed by the Weakly Pareto Optimality of DA For technical details, please refer to the Appendix A.3 of Tang & Yu's paper.

2.5 Observation: For Round r, \(r \geq 1\), the outcome of Round r of EADAM weakly Pareto dominates the outcome of Round (r-1) of EADAM. That is, each student received a weakly better assignment after each iteration of the simplified EADAM.

Proof: Please refer to the Appendix A.3 of Tang & Yu's paper.

Tang & Yu's simplified EADAM are actually outcome equivalent with Kesten's EDAM:

2.6 Theorem: For each instance of school choice with consent problem, the simplified EDAM produced the same outcome as the Kesten's EADAM does.

8 Things on EADAM (1)

Posted on 2021-08-30 Edited on 2021-09-16 In Summary

This post servers as a survey on the Efficiency-adjusted deferred acceptance mechanism (EADAM) firstly introduced by Kesten (2010) in his pioneering paper School choice with consent. I would cover issues on its original definition, different perspectives of implementation, efficiency, stability, equilibrium and simulation.

  1. Kesten(2010)'s original definition

Before introducing the algorithm, we would need one key concept brought by Kesten:

1.1 Definition of Interrupter: Suppose there is a student i, who is tentatively accepted by a school s at step t, and later rejected by school s at step t' (t' > t), meanwhile, if there is some student j, who is rejected by s at step l, \(t \leq l < t'\), then we call student i an interrupter and say that \((i,s)\) form an interrupting pair of step t'.

1.2 Remark: An interrupter can be interpreted as someone who interrupts the favorable settlement among other students without any gain to himself.

1.3 Remark: If the outcome of SOSM is not efficient, then there must be at least one interrupting pair in the DA process. However, the existence of interrupting pair does not imply efficiency loss.

1.4 Example where interrupting pair may not lead to welfare loss:

A problem of 4 students, 3 schools with capacities \(q_1=q_2=1\), \(q_3=2\), priority profile and preference profile are as following:
\(\succ_{s_1}\) \(\succ_{s_2}\) \(\succ_{s_3}\)
\(i_3\) \(i_4\) \(s_1\)
\(i_1\) \(i_3\) \(i_2\)
\(i_2\)
\(P_{i_1}\) \(P_{i_2}\) \(P_{i_3}\) \(P_{i_4}\)
\(s_1\) \(s_1\) \(s_2\) \(\underline{s_2}\)
\(\underline{s_3}\) \(\underline{s_3}\) \(\underline{s_1}\)

The SOSM outcome is underlined in the preference profile and is efficient, while \((i_1,s_1)\) form an interrupting pair.

The essence of the algorithm is to try to recover the welfare loss caused by the interrupters in a right sequence. Due to the constraint efficiency of SOSM1, we know any Pareto improvement would result in priority violation (of the interrupter), hence, it is imperative that we solicit the consent of students before we make any welfare improvement.

1.5 The Efficiency-adjusted deferred acceptance mechanism (EADAM):

Round 0: Run the DA algorithm.

Round k (\(k \geq 1\)): If there are no interrupting pairs or no consenting interrupters, then stops. Otherwise, find the last step of the DA procedure of round \(k-1\), where a consenting interrupter is rejected by the school he forms interrupting pair with. Identify all the interrupting pair with consenting interrupter of that step. For each interrupting pair with consent \((i,s)\), drop school s from i's preference list2 without changing the relative order of the other remaining schools. Rerun DA with the new preference profile.

1.6 Observation: The algorithm terminates after at most |I| × |S| rounds.

Proof: This is because each student can be identified in an interrupting pair for at most |S| times and each round at least one interrupting pair is neutralized.

1.7 Observation: For Round r, \(r \geq 1\), the outcome of Round r of EADAM weakly Pareto dominates the outcome of Round (r-1) of EADAM. That is, each student received a weakly better assignment after each iteration of the EADAM.

Proof: Please refer to Appendix C of Kesten's(2010) paper.

Summary of 'Truncation Strategies in Matching Markets' by Roth & Rothblum (1999)

Posted on 2021-08-28 In Summary

Stability has been argued to be the key to the survival of mechanisms in a two-sided matching markets. While (dominant-strategy) incentive compatbility, also refered as strategy-proofness, is also considered to be of great importance. It is however well known that strategy-proofness is incompatible with stability in the standard two-sided marriage markets. More precisely, under the man-proposing deffered acceptance mechanism, woman may profit from not stating her true preference.

The paper Truncation Strategies in Matching Markets provides an explaination for the success of firm-proposing DA mechanisms in the entry-level labor market, even though they are not strategy-proof. That is, under low information settings, the scope of profitable manipulation for agents is greatly reduced. It also provides a novel appoarch to explore the strategic behaviors of agents under incomplete information setting.

Here is the main take-away (in the form of advice for participants):

  • If the mechanism deployed is firm-proposing deffered acceptance mechanism, and if you have little information on how others make decisions, such that any two firms A, B in the market are symmetric in the sense that, you think it is equally likely that others would prefer A to B or B to A, and it is equally likely that A would ranks you higher than B does or B would rank you higher than A dose, then you can do no better than simply state your true preference or truncate your preference lists without changing the relative orders.
  • Which one is better, truth-telling or truncation? Well, it depends on your tolerance for risk.
  • What if you have some more information? For example, you know which tier the firm lies in, and you know firms in higher tier are preferred to lower tier firms by all other participants. In this case, truncation may no longer be the optimal choice.

Here are some important concepts introduced by the authors:

  • Notations: f for firm, w for worker, \(V =F \cup W\)
  • \(P^{f \leftrightarrow f'}_{S}\): for each worker in S, he changes the relative position of f and f' without changing the others, if f is in S, it changes its preference to (submitted) preference of f', and if f' is in f, it changes its preference to (submitted) preference of f.
  • Preference profile as random variables: \(\widetilde{P}_{-w}\) ranges from all the preference profiles of agents other than w in V.
  • Symmetric information: if the distributions of \(\widetilde{P}_{-w}\), and \((\widetilde{P}_{-w})^{f \leftrightarrow f'}\) coincide, i.e., if for every specific realization of \({P}_{-w}\), \(Pr\{\widetilde{P}_{-w} = P_{-w}\}\) = \(Pr\{(\widetilde{P}_{-w})^{f \leftrightarrow f'} = P_{-w}\}\) , or equivalently, \(Pr\{\widetilde{P}_{-w} = P_{-w}\}\) = \(Pr\{\widetilde{P}_{-w} = P_{-w}^{f \leftrightarrow f'}\}\).
  • A random matching is a random variable whose range is the set of all matchings.

The assumption, where the main results are based on, may not sound too realistic, as it requires every pair of the firms in the market to be indistinguishable, however, it is a nice attempt to study the strategic issues faced by the participants under incomplete informations.

Subsequent and extension of the results include the following papers:

  • Ehlers, Lars, “In Search of Advice for Physicians in Matching Markets Which Use the Deferred Acceptance Algorithm,” Games and Economic Behavior, 48 (2004), 249–270.
  • Ehlers, Lars, “Truncation Strategies in Matching Markets,” Mathematics of Operations Research, 33 (2008), 327–335.

Introducing choice correspondence into school choice model

Posted on 2021-07-30 Edited on 2021-09-16 In Summary

The following are notations introduced by Erdil and Kumano in their paper Prioritizing Diversity in School Choice as an attempt to incorporate more complex priority rule in the school choice problem.

  • N ： set of students, X : set of schools;
  • priority rule : \(\mathcal{C_x}:2^N \rightarrow 2^{2^N}\) s.t. \(S' \subseteq S\) for all \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\)
  • Consistency :
    1. If there exists S such that \(A \cup B \subseteq S\) with \(A \mathcal{C_x}(S)\) and \(B \notin \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), then whenever \(A \cup B \subseteq S'\), we have \(B \notin \mathcal{C_x}(S')\).
    2. If there exists S such that \(A,B \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), then whenever \(A \cup B \subseteq S'\), we have \(A \in \mathcal{C_x}(S')\) if and only if \(B \in \mathcal{C_x}(S')\).
  • Definitely chosen students : \(DC_x(S) = \bigcap_{S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)} S' = \{ i \in S |i \in S'\) for all \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S) \}\)
  • Substitutable A priority structure is substitutable (with ties) if for each \(x \in X\), and for each \(S, T \subseteq N\) with \(S \subseteq T\), the following conditions hold:
    1. for each \(T' \in \mathcal{C_x}(T)\), we have \(T' \cap S \subseteq S'\) for some \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), and
    2. for each \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), we have \(T' \cap S \subseteq S'\) for some \(T' \in \mathcal{C_x}(T)\).
  • Respects priorities : Given students’ preferences R, an assignment \(\mu\) respects priorities C, if:
    1. for each \(i \in N\), \(\mu(i)R_i i\), and
    2. for each \(x \in X\), for every S such that \(\mu^{-1}(x) \subseteq S \subseteq \{i \in N | x R_i \mu(i)\}\), we have \(\mu^{-1}(x) \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\).
  • Pairwise stable :
    1. for each \(i \in N\), \(\mu(i)R_i i\),
    2. \(\mu^{−1}(x) \in \mathcal{C_x}(\mu^{−1}(x))\) for all \(x \in X\),
    3. there is no \((i, x)\in N \times X\) such that \(x P_i \mu(i)\) and \(i \in DC_x(\mu^{−1}(x) \cup {i})\).
  • Acceptant : A priority structure C is acceptant if for each \(x \in X\), for each \(S \subseteq N\), and for each \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\) we have \(|S_0|\) = min{|S|, \(q_x\)}.

An example of an algorithm that converge to stable matching from arbitary matching.

Posted on 2021-07-20 Edited on 2021-09-16 In Examples

In the paper Random Paths to Stability in Two-Sided Matching , Roth and Vande Vate (1990) proposed an algorithm that can recover stability from arbitrary matching. The paper is short and does not contain example of the execution of the algorithm. I provided two examples here:

Notation

Satisfying the blocking pair: For a pair \((m',w')\) that blocks \(\mu\), we say \(\nu\) is obtained by satisfying the blocking pair \((m',w')\) from \(\mu\) if m' and w' are matched at \(\nu\), their original partner \(\mu(m')\) and \(\mu(w')\) each becomes unmatched at \(\nu\) while the others are matched to the same mate under \(\nu\) as they were at \(\mu\).

Decscription of the algorithm

Step 0: Given an arbitrary matching \(\mu_0\), if it is not individually rational, we satisfy all the blocking pair formed by individuals to obtain an individual rational matching \(\mu_1\)
Step 1: If there is no blocking pair for \(\mu_1\), then terminate. Otherwise, pick one blocking pair \((m1,w1)\) and obtain \(\mu_2\) from \(\mu_1\) by satisfying \((m_1,w_1)\), and put the two inside the set \(A(1)\equiv \{m_1,w_1\}\).
Step k (k>1): If there is no blocking pair for \(\mu_k\), then terminate.
Else, if there some men in \(A(k-1)\) forms a blocking pair with a woman \(w_k\) outside \(A(k-1)\), we choose the \(m_j\) to be the man who is \(w_k\)'s most prefered mate in \(A(k-1)\), we obtain \(\mu_{k+1}\) from \(\mu_k\) by satisfying \((m_j,w_k)\) and add \(w_k\) to \(A(k-1)\) to get \(A(k)=A(k-1)\cup\{w_k\}\) and move to next step;
Else if there are some women in \(A(k-1)\) forms a blocking pair with a man \(m_k\) outside \(A(k-1)\), we choose the \(w_j\) to be the woman who is \(m_k\)'s most prefered mate in \(A(k-1)\), we obtain \(\mu_{k+1}\) from \(\mu_k\) by satisfying \((m_k,w_j)\) and add \(m_k\) to \(A(k-1)\) to get \(A(k)=A(k-1)\cup\{m_k\}\) and move to next step;
Else no man or woman in \(A(k-1)\) involved in any blocking for \(\mu(k-1)\), and we find a blocking pair \((m_k,w_k)\) that none of them is in \(A(k-1)\), we obtain \(\mu_{k+1}\) from \(\mu_k\) by satisfying \((m_k,w_k)\) and add \(\{m_k,w_k\}\) to \(A(k-1)\) to get \(A(k)=A(k-1)\cup\{m_k,w_k\}\) and move to next step.

Example 1

Starting from the example used by Knuth (1976) to show existence of cyclic blocking pairs, we have:
P(m1) =w2, w1, w3 P(m2) =w1, w3, w2 P(m3) =w1, w2, w3
P(w1) = m1, m3, m2 P(w2) = m3, m1, m2 P(w3) = m1, m3, m2

and the initial matching is \(\mu_1=\{(m_1,w_1),(m_2,w_2),(m_3,w_3)\}\)

Step 0: \(\mu_1={(w_1,m_1),(w_2,m_2),(w_3,m_3)}\) is individually rational;

Step 1: The blocking pairs are: \(\{(w_2,m_1),(w_2,m_3),(w_3,m_1)\}\), satisfy \((w_2,m_1)\) \(\Rightarrow \mu_2 =\{(w_1,w_1),(w_2,m_1),(m_2,m_2),(w_3,m_3)\}\) and \(A(1)=\{w_2,m_1\}\);

Step 2: The blocking pairs are: \(\{(w_1,m_3),(w_1,m_2),(w_2,m_3)\}\), and \(w_2\) is in \(A(1)\), satisfy \((w_2,m_3)\) \(\Rightarrow \mu_3 =\)$ {(w_1,w_1),(m_1,m_1),(w_2,m_3),(m_2,m_2),(w_3,w_3)} $ and \(A(2)=\{m_1,w_2,m_3\}\);

Step 3: The blocking pairs are: \(\{(w_1,m_3),(w_1,m_2),(w_1,m_1),(w_3,m_1),(w_3,m_2)\}\), \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) is in \(A(2)\) and \(m_1\;P_{w_1}\;m_3\) , satisfy \((m_1,w_1)\) \(\Rightarrow \mu_4 =\)\(\{(w_1,m_1),(w_2,m_3),(m_2,m_2),(w_3,w_3)\}\) and \(A(3)=\{m_1,w_2,m_3,w_1\}\);

Step 4: The blocking pairs are: \(\{(w_3,m_2)\}\), satisfy \((w_3,m_2)\) \(\Rightarrow \mu_5 =\)\(\{(w_1,m_1),(w_2,m_3),(w_3,m_2)\}\) and \(A(3)=\{m_1,w_2,m_3,w_1,w_3,m_2\}\);

Step 5: There is no blocking pair and we achieve a stable matching \(\mu_5 =\{(w_1,m_1),(w_2,m_3),(w_3,m_2)\}\), terminate.

Extension of Thm 2.27 in Roth and Sotomayor (1990) to the context of school choice

Posted on 2021-07-19 Edited on 2021-07-20 In Proofs

  1. Lemma 1 In a school choice problem \((S,I,\succsim,P,q)\) where total capacity is no less than the total number of students and where all students are acceptable for all schools, i.e. \(\sum_{s \in S}q_s \geq |I|\) and \(i \succsim_s \emptyset\) for \(\forall i \in I, \forall s \in S\), there exists at least one school that has not rejected any students under SOSM.

    Proof: Suppose all school has rejected some students under SOSM: Since SOSM terminates in finite steps, there exists a round \(k\), such that there is at least one student rejected in round \(k\) and after \(k\)-th round, no school rejects any students and the algorithm terminates. By our assumption, all students are acceptable, and a school only rejects a student when it runs out of its capacity. This would imply that at round \(k\), all schools are full. However, sincere there is a student who has been rejected in round \(k\) and not matched, this would contradict with our assumption that the number of students is no less than the total number of seats.

  2. Propositions 2 (Weakly Pareto Optimality of SOSM) When all students are acceptable, there is no matching \(\mu\) where all students get a strictly better assignment than his assignment under SOSM, i.e. ther is no \(\mu\) such that \(\mu(i)\; P_i \; SOSM(i)\), \(\forall i \in I\).

    Proof: Case 1: If \(\sum_{s \in S}q_s \leq |I|\), then there are some students unmatched under both \(\mu\) and SOSM, so there is someone who is matched under SOSM but become unmatched under \(\mu\) or remain unmatched under \(\mu\), and hence there exist someone who can't get strictly better assignment.
    Case 2: If \(\sum_{s \in S}q_s \leq |I|\), since all student apply to schools in the order of his preference list, we know all student has been rejected at least once, otherwise there is someone who get his top choice and can't get strictly better assignment. We denote \(S'\) as the set of school that has rejected students under SOSM. We claim that all students must be assigned at some school \(s' \in S'\) under \(\mu\), otherwise he is assigned to a school that has not rejected anyone including him under SOSM, which must be no better than his SOSM assignment. However, by Lemma 1 we know the total capacity of schools in \(S'\) is less than the number of students, i.e. \(\sum_{s' \in S'}q_s < |I|\), hence such a matching \(\mu\) would not be feasible.
