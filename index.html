Core, Stability and Competitive Equilibrium in "Housing market"

Posted on 2022-05-14 Edited on 2022-05-18 In Summary Disqus:

This post cites examples from the paper by Roth and Postlewaite (1977) and aims to help its reader understand and differentiate concepts like Weak/Strong Core, Stability, Pareto-Efficiency and Competitive Equilibrium in the "Housing market" model proposed by Shapley and Scarf (1974).

The Housing Market Model

In Shapley and Scarf (1974)'s model, a group of traders come to the market, each with one object as his endowment. Each trader demand/consume no more than one object. No side payment is allowed and the objects are indivisible, an analogy is housing.

From now on, we denote \(N\) for the set of traders, \(i\) for an arbitrary trader, \(\mathbf{w}=(w_1,w_2,\dots,w_n)\) for their endowments and \(\mathbf{p}\) for the non-negative price vector. \(w_k \succsim_i w_l\) designates that \(i\) likes object k at least as well as object l; \(w_k \succ_i w_l\) designates that i strictly prefers object k to l; and \(w_k \sim_i w_l\) designates that i is indifferent between object k and l. A market is denoted by \(M(\mathbf{w},\succsim)\). An allocation \(\mu=(\mu(i_1),\mu(i_2),\dots,\mu(i_n))\) is a permutation of \(\mathbf{w}\) and maps traders one-to-one to the objects.

The concept

On Core:
  • Def. Strong Domination: We say an allocation \(\mu^x\) strongly dominates \(\mu^y\) if there exists some coalition \(S \subseteq N\), such that:
    1. The allocation is effective, that re-allocation can take place only with the endowments within the coalition: \(\{\mu^{x}(i) |i \in S\}=\{w_i | i \in S\}\)
    2. Each member of the coalition strictly prefers his assignment in \(\mu^x\) to that in \(\mu^y\): \(\mu^x(i) \succ_i \mu^y(i) \text{ for all } i \in S\)
  • Def. Weak Core: The Weak Core of the market \(M(\mathbf{w},\succsim)\) is the set of allocations that are not strongly dominated by any other allocations, i.e. \(\{\mu | \nexists \nu \text{ s.t. }\nu \text{ strongly dominates } \mu \}\)
    • Similarly, we define:
  • Def. Weak Domination: We say an allocation \(\mu^x\) weakly dominates \(\mu^y\) if there exists some coalition \(S \subseteq N\), such that:
    1. The allocation is effective: \(\{\mu^{x}(i) |i \in S\}=\{w_i | i \in S\}\)
    2. Each member of the coalition weakly prefers \(\mu^x\) to \(\mu^y\): \(\mu^x(i) \succsim_i \mu^y(x) \text{ for all } i \in S\)
  • Def. Strong Core: The Weak Core of the market \(M(\mathbf{w},\succsim)\) is the set of allocations that are not weakly dominated by any other allocations.
On Stability:
  • Def. Blocking: A coalition \(S \subseteq N\) blocks an allocation \(\mu\) if there exists some allocation \(\nu\), s.t.
    1. \(\{\nu(i) |i \in S\}=\{\mu(i) | i \in S\}\)
    2. Each member of the coalition strictly prefers his assignment in \(\nu\) to that in \(\mu\): \(\nu(i) \succ_i \mu(i) \text{ for all } i \in S\)
  • Def. Stability: An allocation \(\mu\) is stable if it is not blocked by any coalition.
    Alternatively, we can define stability with the use of core: An allocation \(\mu\) is stable if it is in the core of \(M(\mu,\succsim)\).
On Competitive equilibrium:
  • Def. Efficiency Equilibrium: A pair \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an efficiency equilibrium if no trader \(i\) could sell his assignment \(\mu(i)\) at price \(p_i\) to purchase a more preferred object, i.e. \(\mu(j) \succ_i \mu(i) \Rightarrow p_{\mu(j)} > p_{\mu(i)}\) for all \(i,j\).
  • Def. Efficiency Allocation: An allocation \(\mu\) is said to be efficient if there exists a price vector \(\mathbf{p}\) such that \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an Efficiency Equilibrium.
  • Def. Competitive Equilibrium: A pair \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an competitive equilibrium if it is an efficiency equilibrium and the price of his assignment equals that of his endowment for each trader, i.e. \(p_{\mu(i)}=p_{w_i}\) .
  • Def. Competitive Allocation: An allocation \(\mu\) is said to be competitive if there exists a price vector \(\mathbf{p}\) such that \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an Competitive Equilibrium.

The relationship between concepts

Claim 1 Strong Core of a market is a subset of Weak Core of that market.

This can bee seen from the definition, and here is an example.
Example 1 (from Sec 2 of Roth and Postlewaite (1977)):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\)
\(w_3\) \(w_1\) \(w_2\)
\(w_2\) \(w_2\) \(w_3\)
\(w_1\) \(w_2\) \(w_1\)
Using \((o_1,o_2,o_3)\) to denote \((\mu(1)=o_1,\mu(2)=o_2,\mu(3)=o_3)\),
\((w_3,w_1,w_2)\) strongly dominates every other allocations except \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\), and \(\text{Weak Core}=\{(w_2,w_1,w_3),(w_3,w_1,w_2)\}\),
However, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) is weakly dominated by \((w_3,w_1,w_2)\), hence, \(\text{Strong Core}=\{(w_3,w_1,w_2)\}\).

Lemma 1 The Competitive Equilibrium always exists.

The existence of Competitive Equilibrium can be proven by Gale's constructive approach of Top-Trading Cycle, which is stated in Sec 6 of Shapley and Scarf (1974).
Basically, a Trading Cycle is a group of traders \((i_1,i_2,\dots,i_n=i_0)\) where \(i_k\) gets the object of \(i_{k+1}\)'s among \(k=0,1,\dots,n-1\).

Claim 2 Every competitive allocation is in the Weak Core.

Suppose a competitive equilibrium \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is strongly dominated by another allocation, then there exists a trading cycle \(S=(i_1,i_2,\dots,i_n=i_0)\) whose member can each reallocate his endowment for a better assignment than the current one. Since \(\mathbf{p}\) is efficient, we have \(p_{w_{i_{k+1}}}>p_{\mu(i_k)}\), since it is competitive, we also have \(p_{\mu(i_k)}=p_{w_{i_k}}\), hence we get \(p_{\mu(i_n)}=p_{w_{i_n}}>p_{\mu(i_1)}=p_{w_{i_1}}>p_{\mu(i_2)}=p_{w_{i_2}}>\dots>p_{\mu(i_n)}\), a contradiction!

Claim 3 The Weak Core always exists. While with indifference, the Strong Core may not exists.

By Lemma 1 and Claim 2 we establish the non-emptiness of Weak Core.
The non-existence of Strong Core when indifference is allowed can be demonstrated by the following,
Example 2 (from Sec 5 of Shapley and Scarf (1974)):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\)
\(w_2\) \(w_1 \sim w_3\) \(w_2\)
\(w_1 \sim w_3\) \(w_2\) \(w_1 \sim w_3\)

\(\text{Weak Core}=\{(w_2,w_1,w_3),(w_1,w_3,w_2)\}\),
However, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) weakly dominates \((w_1,w_3,w_2)\) with coalition \(\{i_2,i_3\}\) and \((w_1,w_3,w_2)\) weakly dominates \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) with coalition \(\{i_1,i_2\}\).
Hence, Strong Core = \(\emptyset\).

Claim 4 There may exist an competitive allocation that is not in the Strong Core.

For instance, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) in Example 2 can form a competitive equilibrium together with \(\mathbf{p}\) where \(p_1=p2>p_3\), however, it is weakly dominated by coalition \((w_1,w_3,w_2)\).

Claim 5 There may exist an allocation in the Weak Core that is not stable.

For instance, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) in Example 1 is blocked by coalition \(\{1,3\}\).

Claim 6 Every allocation in the Strong Core is stable.

If a coalition \(S\) could block \(\mu\), with a more preferable re-allocation \(\nu\) on S, then we could construct a allocation \(\mu'\) such that \(\mu'=\nu \text{ on } S\) and \(\mu'=\mu \text{ on } N \setminus S\). \(\mu'\) weakly dominates \(\mu\) with \(N\) being the coalition.

Claim 7 An allocation is stable if and only if it is efficient.

This is stated as Theorem 1 in Roth and Postlewaite (1977). To prove that stability implies efficiency, we can again use Top-Trading Cycle algorithm; The proof of efficiency implying stability is similar to that of Claim 2.

Claim 8 There may exist a stable allocation that is not competitive.

Consider the following example:
Example 3 (Stable/Efficient but not competitive equilibrium):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\) \(\succsim_4\)
\(w_3\) \(w_3\) \(w_4\) \(w_1\)
\(w_2\) \(w_1\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
\(w_1\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
The allocation \((w_2,w_3,w_4,w_1)\) is stable as everyone except \(i_1\) gets his top choice. However, it is not compatible with any competitive price. Because if so, we would have \(p_{w_1}=p_{w_2}=p_{w_3}=p_{w_4}\), however, \(w_3 \succ_{3} w_2=\mu(i_2)\), leading to contradiction!

Claim 9 There may exist a stable allocation that is not in the Weak Core.

Consider the following example:
Example 4 (Stable but not in Core):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\) \(\succsim_4\)
\(w_2\) \(w_4\) \(w_4\) \(w_2\)
\(w_3\) \(w_1\) \(w_1\) \(w_3\)
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
The allocation \((w_2,w_1,w_4,w_3)\) is stable both \(i_1\) and \(i_3\) get their top choices. However, it is not in the Weak Core. Because it is strongly dominated by some allocation where \(\{i_2,i_4\}\) form a coalition to trade with each other.

Especially, when indifference is excluded, we have the nice result:

Claim 10(Theorem 2 in Roth and Postlewaite (1977)) If no trader is indifferent between any goods, then the Strong Core is always non-empty, and contains the unique competitive allocation.

Existence of Bayesian Nash Equilibrium in School Choice Games

Posted on 2022-01-15 Edited on 2022-01-19 In Proofs Disqus:

In this post, I summarized the proof used by He (2017) to show the existence of symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium in Proposition 1 of his paper Gaming the Boston school choice mechanism in Beijing.

Notation and assumption

\(I\) for students and \(S\) for schools; \(\mathbf{L}_{i}=(L^1_i,L^2_1,\dots,L^{|S|}_i)\) is the preference list submitted by student i, and \(L^k_i \in S\) is the k-th choice in his list. \(\mathbf{L}\) denotes the set of all possible list, and the set of all the probibility disrtibution over \(\mathbf{L}\) is denoted by \(\Delta(\mathbf{L}\)) and \(L_{-i}\) denotes the lists submitted by students other than i.

Assumption 1 Students are expected utility maximizers. and we denote their von Neumann-Morgenstern utilities for each school by a vector \([v_{i,s}]_{s \in S}\).

Assumption 2 The valuations \(v_i \equiv [v_{i,s}]_{s \in S}\) follow a distribution \(F\), which is commonly known by all students.
e.g. suppose there are 3 schools, then \(\mathbf{v}_i = \mathbb{R}^3\), for \(V=\{v_{i,1}\in [0,1],v_{i,2}\in [0,1], v_{i,3}\in [0,1]\}\), we have \(Pr(v_{i} \in V)=\int \mathbb{\Large{1}}\{ v \in V\}\,dF(v)\) to denote the probability or the proportion of students whose valuations are in that set.

Assumption 3 The realization of the valuation \(v_i\) is privately known to student \(i\) himself for each \(i \in I\).

Assumption 4 All schools are acceptable for each students1.

Assumption 5 The realization of each student's cardinal preferences are independent.

Given submitted \((L_i,L_{-i})\), we use \(a_{s}(L_i,L_{-i}) \in [0,1]\)2 to denote the probability that student \(i\) is admitted by school \(s\) given the above submitted preference profile, and this probability is determined by the mechanism.

Given submitted \((L_i,L_{-i})\), the expected utility of student \(i\) is
\[V_i(v_i,L_i,L_{-i})=\sum_{s \in S}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})v_{i,s}\]

A mixed strategy \(\sigma\) specifies a probability distribution over all lists, and the mixed strategy profile \(\sigma(\mathbf{v}_i): R^{|S|} \rightarrow \Delta(\mathbf{L})\) specifies what mixed strategy \(i\) should apply contingent on his valuation over school is \(\mathbf{v}_i\).

Given applied mixed strategies \((\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})\), the expected utility of student \(i\) is \[V_i(v_i,\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})=\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_i \in \mathbf{L}}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_i\text{is played under $\sigma_i$)}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i})$)}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})v_{i,s}\]

For simplification of the expression, we write \[A_s(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})=\sum_{L_i \in \mathbf{L}}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_i\text{is played under $\sigma_i$)}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\sigma_{-i}$)}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})\] to denote the probability of being admitted to school \(s\) under the mixed strategies.

And we can simplify the notation to be \(V_i(v_i,\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})= A(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})*v_i\), where \(A(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})=[A_s(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})]_{s \in S}\)

School choice as a Bayesian Game

The school choice can be considered as a Bayesian Game consisting of the tuple \((I,\Omega,(\mathbf{L}_i),(\mathbf{v}_i),(\mathbf{F}_{-i}),(\mathbf{V}_i))\), where
\(I\) is the set of students as a finite set of player;
\(\Omega: \omega \times \mathbf{v}_{-i}\) is the state of the realization of the lottery that determines priority and the realization of others' valuations;
\((\mathbf{L}_{i})\) is the set of actions for each student \(i\), which is his submitted list;
\((\mathbf{v}_i)\) is the set of type for each student \(i\), which is his valuations over the schools;
students hold common prior belief on the distribution of other's valuation as \(F_{-i}(\mathbf{v}_{-i})\)
student i's payoff function is given by \(V_i(v_i,L_i,L_{-i})\) above.

Then, a stundent's interim expected payoff (after knowing his valuation \(v_i\)) is \[\begin{aligned}\underset{\text{over }\omega \times \mathbf{v}_{-i}}{E}[V_i(v_i,\sigma(v_i),\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i}))] &=\int V_i(v_i,\sigma_i(v_i),\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i})) \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i})\\&=\int A(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i}))*v_i \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i}) \\&= \int\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_i \in \mathbf{L}}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_i\text{is played under $\sigma_i$)}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i})$)}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})v_{i,s} \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i}) \end{aligned}\]

Definition 1 A (mixed-strategy) Bayesian Nash Equilibrium is a strategy profile \((\sigma_i(\cdot))_{i \in I}\), such that \[\sigma_i(v_i) = arg\max_{\sigma'_i \in \Delta(\mathbf{L})} \;\;\underset{\text{over }\omega \times \mathbf{v}_{-i}}{E}[V_i(v_i,\sigma(v_i),\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i}))]\] for each \(i\) and \(\forall v_i\).

School choice as a Non-atomic Game

To prove the existence of a symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium of the above game, we can convert it into a nonatomic game, which is a game with complete information and a continuum of non-atomic players. The existence result of non-atomic game is first established by Schmeidler(1973), and then generalized by Khan(1986).

Specially, the school choice game can be considered as a non-atomic game consisting of \(((\mathbf{V},\mathcal{B},\mu),\Delta(\mathbf{L}),\mathbf{X},\mathbf{u})\), where

\((\mathbf{V},\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{V}),\mu)\) is a complete, finite measure space of the types, with \(\mathbf{V}=R^{|S|}\) to be the set of types, \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{V})\) to be the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra of \(\mathbf{v}\), and \(\mu\) is the non-atomic measure3 on \((\mathbf{v},\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{v}))\), s.t. \(\mu(V)=(|I|-1)\int \mathbb{\Large{1}}\{v \in V\}\,dF(v)\);
\(\Delta(\mathbf{L})\) is the set of mixed strategies for each player;4
\(\mathbf{X}: \mathbf{V} \rightarrow \Delta(\mathbf{L})\) is a measurable map that describes the mixed strategy applied by each type;
\(\mathbf{u}(\cdot,\cdot):\mathbf{V} \times \mathcal{L}_1(\mu,X(\cdot)) \rightarrow R^{2^{|S|}}\)5 is the auxiliary utility function, and \[\begin{aligned}u^{j}(v_0,\hat{x})&=\underset{\text{over } \omega \times v_{-i}}E[V(v_i=v_0,L_j,\hat{x}(v_{-i}))]\\ &=\int V(v_0,L_j,\hat{x}(v_{-i}))\,dF_{-i}(v_{-i}) \\ &= \sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}} \int \text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{ is played under $\hat{x}(v_{-i}$))}a_s(L_j,L_{-i})v_{0,s} \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i})\\ &=\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}} \Pi_{k \neq i}( \int \text{Pr(}(L_{-i})_k\text{ is played under $\hat{x}(v_{k}$))}\,dF(v_{k}))a_s(L_j,L_{-i})v_{0,s} \\ \end{aligned}\]6 is the payoff to player of type \(v_0\) when he submits \(L_j\) while the other player act according to the strategy profile described by \(\hat{x}\), and we construct it to coincide with the interim expected utility of a student whose valuation is \(v_i\), submission is \(L_j\) while others' strategies contingent on their realization of valuations are given by \(\hat{x}(v_{-i})=(\hat{x}(v_j))_{j \neq i}\).

Hence, the payoff for a player of type \(v_0\) when he applied strategy \(y=(y_1,\cdots,y_{2^{|I|}})\), while others follows strategy x, can be expressed as \(h_{v_0}(y)=y*u(v_0,x)\), which coincides with the interim expected payoff of student whose type is \(v_0\) and applies strategy \(y\).

Definition 2 An equilibrium of the non-atomic game is the strategy profile \(\hat{x}\), s.t. \[\hat{x}*u(v,\hat{x}) \geq y*u(v,\hat{x}) \;\;\;\; a.e. \forall v\in V\]

Theorem 1(Schmeidler, 1973) A non-atomic game has an equilibrium if it satisfies the following conditions:
  1. for all \(v \in V\), \(u(t,\cdot)\) is continuous in \(\mathcal{L}_1(\mu,X(\cdot))\);
  2. for all \(x \in \Delta(\mathbf{L})\) and \(i,j=1,2,\cdots,2^{|S|}\), the set \(\{v \in V|u^{i}(v,x)>u^{j}(v,x)\}\) is measurable.

For proof, one can refer to the original paper.

Proposition 1 There exists a symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium in the school choice Bayesian Game.

Proof: It suffices to prove the existence of an equilibrium in the non-atomic game.
proof of condition 1:
We have: \[\begin{aligned}&\max_{L \in \mathbf{L}}\, \int |Pr\{\text{L is played under $\hat{x}(v)$}\}-Pr\{\text{L is played under $\hat{y}(v)$}\}| \,d\mu(v)\\ &\leq \int \sum_{L \in \mathbf{L}}\, |Pr\{\text{L is played under $\hat{x}(v)$}\}-Pr\{\text{L is played under $\hat{y}(v)$}\}| \,d\mu(v)=||\hat{x}-\hat{y}||\end{aligned}\] and \[\begin{aligned}&|v^{j}(v,\hat{x})-v^{j}(v,\hat{y})|\\ &=\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}} a_s(L_j,L_{-i})v_{0,s} \{\Pi_{k \neq i}( \int \text{Pr(}(L_{-i})_k\text{ is played under $\hat{x}(v_{k}$))}\,dF(v_{k}))-\Pi_{k \neq i}( \int \text{Pr(}(L_{-i})_k\text{ is played under $\hat{y}(v_{k}$))}\,dF(v_{k}))\}\\ &\leq \sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}} a_s(L_j,L_{-i})v_{0,s} \{\Pi_{k \neq i}( \int \text{Pr(}(L_{-i})_k\text{ is played under $\hat{y}(v_{k}$))}\,dF(v_{k})+||\hat{x}-\hat{y}||)-\Pi_{k \neq i}( \int \text{Pr(}(L_{-i})_k\text{ is played under $\hat{y}(v_{k}$))}\,dF(v_{k}))\} \end{aligned}\] hence, we can take \(|\hat{x}-\hat{y}|\) arbitrarily small to make \(|v^{j}(v,\hat{x})-v^{j}(v,\hat{y})|\) close enough to 0 and \(||v(v,\hat{x})-v(v,\hat{y})||\) close enough to 0.


proof of condition 2: Since the integral \(\int \text{Pr(}(L_{-i})_k\text{ is played under $\hat{x}(v_{k})$)}\,dF(v_{k})\) average out \(v\) and is a constant, we know \(u^{j}(v_0,\hat{x})=C*v_0\) is linear in \(v_0\) with some constant coefficient, the set \(\{v \in V|u^{i}(v,x)>u^{j}(v,x)\}=\{v \in V|(C^{i}-C^{j})*v>0)\}\) is measurable.

A student can weakly increase her chance of being assigned to a school by ranking it higher in her list under SOSM and BM.

Posted on 2022-01-08 In Proofs Disqus:

In this post, I provide my (a bit verbose) proof of a monotonicity property shared by both Student-proposing Deferred Acceptance and Immediate Acceptance mechanisms. That is, a student can weakly increase her chance of being assigned to a school by ranking it higher in her list.

Notations

\(I\) for the set of students and \(i\) for a general student, the Ranked Order List submitted by student \(i\) is denoted as \(L_i\), the lists submitted by others are denoted as \(L_{-i}\); \(S\) for the set of schools and \(s\) for a general school, \(q_s\) for the capacity of school \(s\), the priority list over students at school \(s\) is denoted as \(P_i\); SOSM for Student optimal stable mechanism and BM for Boston Mechanism, \(\mu^{M}(i,L_i,L_{-i})\) to be the assignment of student i under mechanism M; we say a student \(i\) moves up a school \(s\) in her list if she ranks \(s\) higher while keeping the relative ranking among the remaining schools unchanged, that is \(|\{s'|s' L_i s \}|<|\{s'|s' L'_i s \}|\) and \(s' L_i s \Leftrightarrow s' L'_i s\).

Lemma 1

Given \(\forall \; (I,S,\mathbf{q},\mathbf{P},L_{-i})\), if \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L_i,L_{-i})=s\) and if i moves up s in her list to have \(L'_i\), then \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\).

Proof by contradiction:
Suppose \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s' \neq s\).
Suppose i applies to s at step t under \(\mu^{SOSM}(L_i,L_{-i})\) and at step \(t'<t\) under \(\mu^{SOSM}(L'_i,L_{-i})\), we know the steps ahead of \(t'\) are identical under the two cases1, such that the application of other students are the same for \(i' \neq i\).

  1. If \(s L_i s'\), then it implies at some step \(k\) before the algorithm terminates, there is a student \(a\) applying to \(s\) under \(\mu'\), who never applies to \(s\) under \(\mu(L)\), and her priority is higher than \(i\), i.e. \(a P_s i\). This must be caused2 by some other student \(b\), who applied to a's original assignment \(\mu(a,Li,L_{-i})\), at a step \(k' < k\). This must be caused by another student \(c\), who applied to b's original assignment \(\mu(b,Li,L_{-i})\), at a step \(k'' < k'\)...

Stable matchings can be represented as extreme points of the solutions of a system of linear inequalities

Posted on 2021-12-22 Edited on 2021-12-24 In Summary Disqus:

This post serves as a summary of Section 3.2.4 in the book Two-Sided Matching by Roth and Sotomayor(1990). From the last post, we know that the set of stable matchings can be thought as set of fixed points for some functions on a complete lattice, in this post, we would show a similar and as surprising result, that the set of stable matchings can be characterized as the set of extreme points of a system of linear constraints.

An extreme points of a convex set \(C\) is a point that doesn't line in any line segment entirely contained in the set \(C\). That is, for \(x \in C\) being an extreme point \(\nexists y,z \in C\) s.t. \(x=\alpha y + (1- \alpha)z\) for some \(\alpha \in (0,1)\), or equivalently, \(x=\alpha y + (1- \alpha)z\) \(\Rightarrow \alpha = 0 \text{ or } 1\).

In a market of \(M \times W\), we can represent fractional matchings by a \(|M| \times |W|\) matrix \(\mathbf{x}\), which satisfies the following constraints:
\(\sum_{j}x_{mj} \leq 1\) for all m in M (1);
\(\sum_{i}x_{jw} \leq 1\) for all w in W (2);
\(x_{mw} \geq 0\) for all m in M and all w in W (3);
One way is to interpret \(\mathbf{x}\) as lotteries and to consider \(x_{mw}\) as the probability that m is matched to w. The other way is to interpret \(\mathbf{x}\) as time-sharing arrangements and to consider \(x_{mw}\) as the time that m is matched with w.

A matrix \(\mathbf{x}\) is a (configuration of) matching if there is matching \(\mu\) s.t. \(x_{mw}=1\) if.f. \(\mu(m)=w\). We can see that every integer solutions of the above constraints (1,2,3) is a matching, where \(\sum_{j}x_{mj} = 0\) means m is unmatched and \(\sum_{i}x_{jw} = 0\) means w is unmatched.

For a matching to be individually rational, we need to impose the constraint to make sure no one is matched with unacceptable partner. Denoting \(A \equiv \{(m,w)|w \succ_m w \& m \succ_w w\}\), we can express the constraints as:
\(x_{mw}=0\) for \((m,w) \in M \times W \setminus A\) (4);

For stability, we need one more constraint:
\(\underset{j \succ_m w}{\sum}x_{mj} + \underset{i \succ_w m}{\sum}x_{iw} + x_{mw} \geq 1\) for all \((m,w) \in A\) (5)1;

It is not difficult to see every integer solution of the constraints (1-5) is an extreme points. Because if not, we can find \(\mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{z}\) s.t. \(0<\alpha y_{ij} + (1- \alpha) z_{ij}<1\) for some \((i,j)\) violating that every element of \(\mathbf{x}\) is an integer.

To show that every extreme points of the set of solutions for the constraints (1-5) is an integer solution, we need to construct an auxiliary matrix \(\mathbf{z(x)}=\mathbf{\bar{z}(x)}-\mathbf{\underline{z}(x)}\), where
\(\bar{z}(x)_{mw}=1\) if \(w=\bar{x}(m)\) and \(\bar{z}(x)_{mw}=0\) otherwise;
\(\underline{z}(x)_{mw}=1\) if \(w=\underline{x}(m)\) and \(\underline{z}(x)_{mw}=0\) otherwise;
\(\bar{x}(m)\equiv \underset{w.r.t \succ_m}{\max}\{w \in W| x_{mw}>0\}\) is man's most preferred woman among \(\{w \in W s.t. x_{mw}>0\}\);
\(\underline{x}(m)\equiv \underset{w.r.t \succ_m}{\min}\{w \in W| x_{mw}>0\}\) is man's least preferred woman among \(\{w \in W s.t. x_{mw}>0\}\).

such that for any extreme solution \(\mathbf{x}\), we can find small enough \(\delta\), s.t. both \(\mathbf{x}-\delta \mathbf{z(x)}\) and \(\mathbf{x}+\delta \mathbf{z(x)}\) would solve (1-5), while \(\mathbf{x} = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}+\delta \mathbf{z(x)}) + \frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{x}-\delta \mathbf{z(x)})\), hence, we must require \(\mathbf{z(x)}=0\). This would imply \(\bar{x}(m)=\underline{x}(m)\) for all m, and we know \(\mathbf{x}\) is an integer solution.

For details of the proof of the statement underline above, one can refer to the book or the paper by Vande Vate (1992).

Roth, A., & Sotomayor, M. (1990). The structure of the set of stable matchings. In Two-Sided Matching: A Study in Game-Theoretic Modeling and Analysis (Econometric Society Monographs, pp. 54-77). Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. doi:10.1017/CCOL052139015X.003

Existence and lattice structure of stable matchings can be proven by Tarski’s fixed point theorem.

Posted on 2021-12-20 Edited on 2021-12-24 In Summary Disqus:

This post serves as a summary of the paper On a characterization of stable matchings by Hiroyuki Adachi (2000).

Tarski's fixed point theorem states that, For\((L,\leq)\) being a complete lattice and \(f:L \to L\) being a monotone function w.r.t. \(\leq\), the set of fixed points of \(f\) forms a complete lattice.

The key of the proof is to identify a complete lattice structure where stable matchings can be considered as fixed points of some increasing function. And it turned out, we can consider pre-matchings to be the set \(L\) and common preference of man to be the partial order.

Informally, pre-matching is a looser concept than matching. A matching \(\mu: M \cup W \to M \cup W\) is a one-to-one correspondence and is onto itself of order 2(\(\mu(\mu(x))=x\)) as we require a man to be matched to the woman who is matched to him. Pre-matching only requires it to be a function, mathematically, a pre-matchinng can be described by a pair of function \(\nu \equiv (\nu_M,\nu_W)\) where \(\nu_M : M \to M \cup W\) s.t. if \(\nu_M(m) \notin W\) then \(\nu_M(m)=m\), and \(\nu_W : W \to M \cup W\) s.t. if \(\nu_W(w) \notin M\) then \(\nu_W(w)=w\). The set of all such pair of functions is called \(pre-matchings\), i.e. \(V:=V_M \times V_W\).

Common preference of man over pre-matching is a partial order such that a prematching is prefered to the other if every man prefers his pre-matched partner to that in the other pre-matching, mathematically,
\(\geq_M\) on \(V_M\) specifies \(\nu_M \geq_M \nu_M'\) if.f. \(\nu_M(m) \geq_M \nu_M'(m)\) for all m;
\(\geq_W\) on \(V_W\) specifies \(\nu_W \geq_W \nu_W'\) if.f. \(\nu_W(w) \geq_W \nu_W'(w)\) for all m;
\(\geqslant_M\) on \(V\) specifies \(\nu_M \geqslant_M \nu_M'\) if.f. \(\nu_M \geq_M \nu_M'\) and \(\nu_M \leq_W \nu_M'\).

\((V,\geqslant_M)\) is a lattice. For \(\nu_1=(\nu_{1M},\nu_{1W})=((\nu_{1m})_{m \in M},(\nu_{1w})_{w \in W})\), \(\nu_2=(\nu_{2M},\nu_{2W})=((\nu_{2m})_{m \in M},(\nu_{2w})_{w \in W}) \in V\), \(\nu_1 \wedge \nu_2 = ((\nu_{1m} \wedge_m \nu_{2m})_{m \in M},(\nu_{1w} \vee_w \nu_{2w})_{w \in W})\) and \(\nu_1 \vee \nu_2 = ((\nu_{1m} \vee_m \nu_{2m})_{m \in M},(\nu_{1w} \wedge_w \nu_{2w})_{w \in W})\)1.

\((V,\geqslant_M)\) is a complete lattice because it is finite.

All stable matchings can be considered as pre-matching that solves the following two equations
\(\nu_M(m)= \underset{w.r.t. \geq_m}{max}\{\{w \in W| m\geq_w \nu_W(w)\} \cup \{m\}\}\) for all \(m\in M\) (1)
\(\nu_W(w)= \underset{w.r.t. \geq_w}{max}\{\{m \in M| m\geq_m \nu_M(m)\} \cup \{w\}\}\) for all \(w\in W\) (2)
For proofs, one can refer to Prop. 3.4 of the original paper.

Consider a mapping \(T\equiv(T_1,T_2)\) with \(T_1: V \to V_M\), such that \(T_1(\nu)\) is the solution of (1), while \(T_2(\nu)\) is the solution of (2). It is not hard to show that \(T\) is non-decreasing in \(\nu\) w.r.t. \(\geqslant_M\).

A Stable matching \(\mu\) is a fixed point of \(T\) as it solves both equation (1,2) simultaneously. So, according to Tarski's fixed point theorem, the set of stable matchings (\(\hat{V}\) that solves (1,2)) is non-empty and \((\hat{V},\geqslant_M)\) is a complete lattice.

Reference

Adachi, H. (2000). On a characterization of stable matchings. Economics Letters, 68, 43-49.

  1. Here I am abusing notations a bit, \(w_1 \wedge_m w_2\) means m's less preferable woman among \(w_1,w_2\), while \(w_1 \vee_m w_2\) means m's more preferable woman among the two.↩

  1. One perspective provided by Tang & Yu (2014)

Focusing on each student's incentive to give consent or not in the school choice with consent model we discussed last time, Tang & Yu provided a new prospective of EADAM. It is reasoned that in order not to disincentivize a student to give consent, we should violate his priorities to allow improvement of others' assignment only when the assignment of this student can not be improved any more, so that his chance of getting into a better school would not be hurted by his consent. To identify which students are not Pareto-improvable given the outcome of DA, we could first identify which schools are under-demanded.

2.1 Definition of Under-demanded school: A school s is under-demanded at a matching \(\mu\) if no student prefers it to his assignment under \(\mu\), that is, \(\nexists i\), such that \(s \succ_{i} \mu(i)\).

The following lemma tells us that students who are matched with under-demanded school in a DA matching are not Pareto-improvable.

2.2 Lemma: At a DA matching, all students who are matched with under-demanded schools are not Pareto-improvable. Proof: Actually, this result also hold for any non-wasteful matching mechanisms, and this is because Pareto-improvements can only take the form of trading when the matching is non-wasteful. The intuition behind is straightforward, that is, no student would be willing to trade his assignment for a under-demanded school. For technical details, please refer to the Appendix A.1 of Tang & Yu's paper.

2.3 The simplified Efficiency-adjusted deferred acceptance mechanism (simplified EADAM):

Round 0: Run the DA algorithm.

Round k (\(k \geq 1\)): 1. Identify all the under-demanded schools at the (k-1)-th round, settle these schools as the final assignment for students who are matched with these schools, remove these schools and the matched students; 2. For each removed student i, check whether he gives consent or not. If not, for each school s that is preferred to his matching \(\mu(i)\), we modify the priority of s such that any student j who has a lower priority than i is considered as unacceptable for s1; 3. 2.4 Observation: The algorithm terminates after at most (|S|+1) rounds.

Proof: This is ensured by the existence of under-demanded school at the end of each round, which is guaranteed by the Weakly Pareto Optimality of DA For technical details, please refer to the Appendix A.3 of Tang & Yu's paper.

Proof: Please refer to the Appendix A.3 of Tang & Yu's paper.

8 Things on EADAM (1)

Posted on 2021-08-30 Edited on 2021-09-16 In Summary Disqus:

This post servers as a survey on the Efficiency-adjusted deferred acceptance mechanism (EADAM) firstly introduced by Kesten (2010) in his pioneering paper School choice with consent. I would cover issues on its original definition, different perspectives of implementation, efficiency, stability, equilibrium and simulation.

  1. Kesten(2010)'s original definition

Before introducing the algorithm, we would need one key concept brought by Kesten:

1.1 Definition of Interrupter: Suppose there is a student i, who is tentatively accepted by a school s at step t, and later rejected by school s at step t' (t' > t), meanwhile, if there is some student j, who is rejected by s at step l, \(t \leq l < t'\), then we call student i an interrupter and say that \((i,s)\) form an interrupting pair of step t'.

1.2 Remark: An interrupter can be interpreted as someone who interrupts the favorable settlement among other students without any gain to himself.

1.3 Remark: If the outcome of SOSM is not efficient, then there must be at least one interrupting pair in the DA process. However, the existence of interrupting pair does not imply efficiency loss.

1.4 Example where interrupting pair may not lead to welfare loss:

A problem of 4 students, 3 schools with capacities \(q_1=q_2=1\), \(q_3=2\), priority profile and preference profile are as following:
\(\succ_{s_1}\) \(\succ_{s_2}\) \(\succ_{s_3}\)
\(i_3\) \(i_4\) \(s_1\)
\(i_1\) \(i_3\) \(i_2\)
\(i_2\)
\(P_{i_1}\) \(P_{i_2}\) \(P_{i_3}\) \(P_{i_4}\)
\(s_1\) \(s_1\) \(s_2\) \(\underline{s_2}\)
\(\underline{s_3}\) \(\underline{s_3}\) \(\underline{s_1}\)

The SOSM outcome is underlined in the preference profile and is efficient, while \((i_1,s_1)\) form an interrupting pair.

The essence of the algorithm is to try to recover the welfare loss caused by the interrupters in a right sequence. Due to the constraint efficiency of SOSM1, we know any Pareto improvement would result in priority violation (of the interrupter), hence, it is imperative that we solicit the consent of students before we make any welfare improvement.

1.5 The Efficiency-adjusted deferred acceptance mechanism (EADAM):

Round 0: Run the DA algorithm.

Round k (\(k \geq 1\)): If there are no interrupting pairs or no consenting interrupters, then stops. Otherwise, find the last step of the DA procedure of round \(k-1\), where a consenting interrupter is rejected by the school he forms interrupting pair with. Identify all the interrupting pair with consenting interrupter of that step. For each interrupting pair with consent \((i,s)\), drop school s from i's preference list2 without changing the relative order of the other remaining schools. Rerun DA with the new preference profile.

1.6 Observation: The algorithm terminates after at most |I| × |S| rounds.

Proof: This is because each student can be identified in an interrupting pair for at most |S| times and each round at least one interrupting pair is neutralized.

1.7 Observation: For Round r, \(r \geq 1\), the outcome of Round r of EADAM weakly Pareto dominates the outcome of Round (r-1) of EADAM. That is, each student received a weakly better assignment after each iteration of the EADAM.

Proof: Please refer to Appendix C of Kesten's(2010) paper.

Summary of 'Truncation Strategies in Matching Markets' by Roth & Rothblum (1999)

Posted on 2021-08-28 In Summary Disqus:

Stability has been argued to be the key to the survival of mechanisms in a two-sided matching markets. While (dominant-strategy) incentive compatbility, also refered as strategy-proofness, is also considered to be of great importance. It is however well known that strategy-proofness is incompatible with stability in the standard two-sided marriage markets. More precisely, under the man-proposing deffered acceptance mechanism, woman may profit from not stating her true preference.

The paper Truncation Strategies in Matching Markets provides an explaination for the success of firm-proposing DA mechanisms in the entry-level labor market, even though they are not strategy-proof. That is, under low information settings, the scope of profitable manipulation for agents is greatly reduced. It also provides a novel appoarch to explore the strategic behaviors of agents under incomplete information setting.

Here is the main take-away (in the form of advice for participants):

  • If the mechanism deployed is firm-proposing deffered acceptance mechanism, and if you have little information on how others make decisions, such that any two firms A, B in the market are symmetric in the sense that, you think it is equally likely that others would prefer A to B or B to A, and it is equally likely that A would ranks you higher than B does or B would rank you higher than A dose, then you can do no better than simply state your true preference or truncate your preference lists without changing the relative orders.
  • Which one is better, truth-telling or truncation? Well, it depends on your tolerance for risk.
  • What if you have some more information? For example, you know which tier the firm lies in, and you know firms in higher tier are preferred to lower tier firms by all other participants. In this case, truncation may no longer be the optimal choice.

Here are some important concepts introduced by the authors:

  • Notations: f for firm, w for worker, \(V =F \cup W\)
  • \(P^{f \leftrightarrow f'}_{S}\): for each worker in S, he changes the relative position of f and f' without changing the others, if f is in S, it changes its preference to (submitted) preference of f', and if f' is in f, it changes its preference to (submitted) preference of f.
  • Preference profile as random variables: \(\widetilde{P}_{-w}\) ranges from all the preference profiles of agents other than w in V.
  • Symmetric information: if the distributions of \(\widetilde{P}_{-w}\), and \((\widetilde{P}_{-w})^{f \leftrightarrow f'}\) coincide, i.e., if for every specific realization of \({P}_{-w}\), \(Pr\{\widetilde{P}_{-w} = P_{-w}\}\) = \(Pr\{(\widetilde{P}_{-w})^{f \leftrightarrow f'} = P_{-w}\}\) , or equivalently, \(Pr\{\widetilde{P}_{-w} = P_{-w}\}\) = \(Pr\{\widetilde{P}_{-w} = P_{-w}^{f \leftrightarrow f'}\}\).
  • A random matching is a random variable whose range is the set of all matchings.

The assumption, where the main results are based on, may not sound too realistic, as it requires every pair of the firms in the market to be indistinguishable, however, it is a nice attempt to study the strategic issues faced by the participants under incomplete informations.

Subsequent and extension of the results include the following papers:

  • Ehlers, Lars, “In Search of Advice for Physicians in Matching Markets Which Use the Deferred Acceptance Algorithm,” Games and Economic Behavior, 48 (2004), 249–270.
  • Ehlers, Lars, “Truncation Strategies in Matching Markets,” Mathematics of Operations Research, 33 (2008), 327–335.

Introducing choice correspondence into school choice model

Posted on 2021-07-30 Edited on 2021-09-16 In Summary Disqus:

The following are notations introduced by Erdil and Kumano in their paper Prioritizing Diversity in School Choice as an attempt to incorporate more complex priority rule in the school choice problem.

  • N ： set of students, X : set of schools;
  • priority rule : \(\mathcal{C_x}:2^N \rightarrow 2^{2^N}\) s.t. \(S' \subseteq S\) for all \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\)
  • Consistency :
    1. If there exists S such that \(A \cup B \subseteq S\) with \(A \mathcal{C_x}(S)\) and \(B \notin \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), then whenever \(A \cup B \subseteq S'\), we have \(B \notin \mathcal{C_x}(S')\).
    2. If there exists S such that \(A,B \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), then whenever \(A \cup B \subseteq S'\), we have \(A \in \mathcal{C_x}(S')\) if and only if \(B \in \mathcal{C_x}(S')\).
  • Definitely chosen students : \(DC_x(S) = \bigcap_{S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)} S' = \{ i \in S |i \in S'\) for all \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S) \}\)
  • Substitutable A priority structure is substitutable (with ties) if for each \(x \in X\), and for each \(S, T \subseteq N\) with \(S \subseteq T\), the following conditions hold:
    1. for each \(T' \in \mathcal{C_x}(T)\), we have \(T' \cap S \subseteq S'\) for some \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), and
    2. for each \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\), we have \(T' \cap S \subseteq S'\) for some \(T' \in \mathcal{C_x}(T)\).
  • Respects priorities : Given students’ preferences R, an assignment \(\mu\) respects priorities C, if:
    1. for each \(i \in N\), \(\mu(i)R_i i\), and
    2. for each \(x \in X\), for every S such that \(\mu^{-1}(x) \subseteq S \subseteq \{i \in N | x R_i \mu(i)\}\), we have \(\mu^{-1}(x) \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\).
  • Pairwise stable :
    1. for each \(i \in N\), \(\mu(i)R_i i\),
    2. \(\mu^{−1}(x) \in \mathcal{C_x}(\mu^{−1}(x))\) for all \(x \in X\),
    3. there is no \((i, x)\in N \times X\) such that \(x P_i \mu(i)\) and \(i \in DC_x(\mu^{−1}(x) \cup {i})\).
  • Acceptant : A priority structure C is acceptant if for each \(x \in X\), for each \(S \subseteq N\), and for each \(S' \in \mathcal{C_x}(S)\) we have \(|S_0|\) = min{|S|, \(q_x\)}.
