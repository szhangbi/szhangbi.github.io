Core, Stability and Competitive Equilibrium in "Housing market"

Posted on 2022-05-14 Edited on 2022-05-18 In Summary Views: Disqus:

This post cites examples from the paper by Roth and Postlewaite (1977) and aims to help its reader understand and differentiate concepts like Weak/Strong Core, Stability, Pareto-Efficiency and Competitive Equilibrium in the "Housing market" model proposed by Shapley and Scarf (1974).

The Housing Market Model

In Shapley and Scarf (1974)'s model, a group of traders come to the market, each with one object as his endowment. Each trader demand/consume no more than one object. No side payment is allowed and the objects are indivisible, an analogy is housing.

From now on, we denote \(N\) for the set of traders, \(i\) for an arbitrary trader, \(\mathbf{w}=(w_1,w_2,\dots,w_n)\) for their endowments and \(\mathbf{p}\) for the non-negative price vector. \(w_k \succsim_i w_l\) designates that \(i\) likes object k at least as well as object l; \(w_k \succ_i w_l\) designates that i strictly prefers object k to l; and \(w_k \sim_i w_l\) designates that i is indifferent between object k and l. A market is denoted by \(M(\mathbf{w},\succsim)\). An allocation \(\mu=(\mu(i_1),\mu(i_2),\dots,\mu(i_n))\) is a permutation of \(\mathbf{w}\) and maps traders one-to-one to the objects.

The concept

On Core:
  • Def. Strong Domination: We say an allocation \(\mu^x\) strongly dominates \(\mu^y\) if there exists some coalition \(S \subseteq N\), such that:
    1. The allocation is effective, that re-allocation can take place only with the endowments within the coalition: \(\{\mu^{x}(i) |i \in S\}=\{w_i | i \in S\}\)
    2. Each member of the coalition strictly prefers his assignment in \(\mu^x\) to that in \(\mu^y\): \(\mu^x(i) \succ_i \mu^y(i) \text{ for all } i \in S\)
  • Def. Weak Core: The Weak Core of the market \(M(\mathbf{w},\succsim)\) is the set of allocations that are not strongly dominated by any other allocations, i.e. \(\{\mu | \nexists \nu \text{ s.t. }\nu \text{ strongly dominates } \mu \}\)
    • Similarly, we define:
  • Def. Weak Domination: We say an allocation \(\mu^x\) weakly dominates \(\mu^y\) if there exists some coalition \(S \subseteq N\), such that:
    1. The allocation is effective: \(\{\mu^{x}(i) |i \in S\}=\{w_i | i \in S\}\)
    2. Each member of the coalition weakly prefers \(\mu^x\) to \(\mu^y\): \(\mu^x(i) \succsim_i \mu^y(x) \text{ for all } i \in S\)
  • Def. Strong Core: The Weak Core of the market \(M(\mathbf{w},\succsim)\) is the set of allocations that are not weakly dominated by any other allocations.
On Stability:
  • Def. Blocking: A coalition \(S \subseteq N\) blocks an allocation \(\mu\) if there exists some allocation \(\nu\), s.t.
    1. \(\{\nu(i) |i \in S\}=\{\mu(i) | i \in S\}\)
    2. Each member of the coalition strictly prefers his assignment in \(\nu\) to that in \(\mu\): \(\nu(i) \succ_i \mu(i) \text{ for all } i \in S\)
  • Def. Stability: An allocation \(\mu\) is stable if it is not blocked by any coalition.
    Alternatively, we can define stability with the use of core: An allocation \(\mu\) is stable if it is in the core of \(M(\mu,\succsim)\).
On Competitive equilibrium:
  • Def. Efficiency Equilibrium: A pair \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an efficiency equilibrium if no trader \(i\) could sell his assignment \(\mu(i)\) at price \(p_i\) to purchase a more preferred object, i.e. \(\mu(j) \succ_i \mu(i) \Rightarrow p_{\mu(j)} > p_{\mu(i)}\) for all \(i,j\).
  • Def. Efficiency Allocation: An allocation \(\mu\) is said to be efficient if there exists a price vector \(\mathbf{p}\) such that \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an Efficiency Equilibrium.
  • Def. Competitive Equilibrium: A pair \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an competitive equilibrium if it is an efficiency equilibrium and the price of his assignment equals that of his endowment for each trader, i.e. \(p_{\mu(i)}=p_{w_i}\) .
  • Def. Competitive Allocation: An allocation \(\mu\) is said to be competitive if there exists a price vector \(\mathbf{p}\) such that \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is an Competitive Equilibrium.

The relationship between concepts

Claim 1 Strong Core of a market is a subset of Weak Core of that market.

This can bee seen from the definition, and here is an example.
Example 1 (from Sec 2 of Roth and Postlewaite (1977)):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\)
\(w_3\) \(w_1\) \(w_2\)
\(w_2\) \(w_2\) \(w_3\)
\(w_1\) \(w_2\) \(w_1\)
Using \((o_1,o_2,o_3)\) to denote \((\mu(1)=o_1,\mu(2)=o_2,\mu(3)=o_3)\),
\((w_3,w_1,w_2)\) strongly dominates every other allocations except \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\), and \(\text{Weak Core}=\{(w_2,w_1,w_3),(w_3,w_1,w_2)\}\),
However, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) is weakly dominated by \((w_3,w_1,w_2)\), hence, \(\text{Strong Core}=\{(w_3,w_1,w_2)\}\).

Lemma 1 The Competitive Equilibrium always exists.

The existence of Competitive Equilibrium can be proven by Gale's constructive approach of Top-Trading Cycle, which is stated in Sec 6 of Shapley and Scarf (1974).
Basically, a Trading Cycle is a group of traders \((i_1,i_2,\dots,i_n=i_0)\) where \(i_k\) gets the object of \(i_{k+1}\)'s among \(k=0,1,\dots,n-1\).

Claim 2 Every competitive allocation is in the Weak Core.

Suppose a competitive equilibrium \((\mathbf{p},\mu)\) is strongly dominated by another allocation, then there exists a trading cycle \(S=(i_1,i_2,\dots,i_n=i_0)\) whose member can each reallocate his endowment for a better assignment than the current one. Since \(\mathbf{p}\) is efficient, we have \(p_{w_{i_{k+1}}}>p_{\mu(i_k)}\), since it is competitive, we also have \(p_{\mu(i_k)}=p_{w_{i_k}}\), hence we get \(p_{\mu(i_n)}=p_{w_{i_n}}>p_{\mu(i_1)}=p_{w_{i_1}}>p_{\mu(i_2)}=p_{w_{i_2}}>\dots>p_{\mu(i_n)}\), a contradiction!

Claim 3 The Weak Core always exists. While with indifference, the Strong Core may not exists.

By Lemma 1 and Claim 2 we establish the non-emptiness of Weak Core.
The non-existence of Strong Core when indifference is allowed can be demonstrated by the following,
Example 2 (from Sec 5 of Shapley and Scarf (1974)):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\)
\(w_2\) \(w_1 \sim w_3\) \(w_2\)
\(w_1 \sim w_3\) \(w_2\) \(w_1 \sim w_3\)

\(\text{Weak Core}=\{(w_2,w_1,w_3),(w_1,w_3,w_2)\}\),
However, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) weakly dominates \((w_1,w_3,w_2)\) with coalition \(\{i_2,i_3\}\) and \((w_1,w_3,w_2)\) weakly dominates \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) with coalition \(\{i_1,i_2\}\).
Hence, Strong Core = \(\emptyset\).

Claim 4 There may exist an competitive allocation that is not in the Strong Core.

For instance, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) in Example 2 can form a competitive equilibrium together with \(\mathbf{p}\) where \(p_1=p2>p_3\), however, it is weakly dominated by coalition \((w_1,w_3,w_2)\).

Claim 5 There may exist an allocation in the Weak Core that is not stable.

For instance, \((w_2,w_1,w_3)\) in Example 1 is blocked by coalition \(\{1,3\}\).

Claim 6 Every allocation in the Strong Core is stable.

If a coalition \(S\) could block \(\mu\), with a more preferable re-allocation \(\nu\) on S, then we could construct a allocation \(\mu'\) such that \(\mu'=\nu \text{ on } S\) and \(\mu'=\mu \text{ on } N \setminus S\). \(\mu'\) weakly dominates \(\mu\) with \(N\) being the coalition.

Claim 7 An allocation is stable if and only if it is efficient.

This is stated as Theorem 1 in Roth and Postlewaite (1977). To prove that stability implies efficiency, we can again use Top-Trading Cycle algorithm; The proof of efficiency implying stability is similar to that of Claim 2.

Claim 8 There may exist a stable allocation that is not competitive.

Consider the following example:
Example 3 (Stable/Efficient but not competitive equilibrium):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\) \(\succsim_4\)
\(w_3\) \(w_3\) \(w_4\) \(w_1\)
\(w_2\) \(w_1\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
\(w_1\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
The allocation \((w_2,w_3,w_4,w_1)\) is stable as everyone except \(i_1\) gets his top choice. However, it is not compatible with any competitive price. Because if so, we would have \(p_{w_1}=p_{w_2}=p_{w_3}=p_{w_4}\), however, \(w_3 \succ_{3} w_2=\mu(i_2)\), leading to contradiction!

Claim 9 There may exist a stable allocation that is not in the Weak Core.

Consider the following example:
Example 4 (Stable but not in Core):

\(\succsim_1\) \(\succsim_2\) \(\succsim_3\) \(\succsim_4\)
\(w_2\) \(w_4\) \(w_4\) \(w_2\)
\(w_3\) \(w_1\) \(w_1\) \(w_3\)
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
The allocation \((w_2,w_1,w_4,w_3)\) is stable both \(i_1\) and \(i_3\) get their top choices. However, it is not in the Weak Core. Because it is strongly dominated by some allocation where \(\{i_2,i_4\}\) form a coalition to trade with each other.

Especially, when indifference is excluded, we have the nice result:

Claim 10(Theorem 2 in Roth and Postlewaite (1977)) If no trader is indifferent between any goods, then the Strong Core is always non-empty, and contains the unique competitive allocation.
