Existence of Bayesian Nash Equilibrium in School Choice Games

Posted on 2022-01-15 Edited on 2022-01-17 In Proofs Views: Disqus:

In this post, I summarized the proof used by He (2017) to show the existence of symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium in Proposition 1 of his paper Gaming the Boston school choice mechanism in Beijing.

Notation and assumption

\(I\) for students and \(S\) for schools; \(\mathbf{L}_{i}=(L^1_i,L^2_1,\dots,L^{|S|}_i)\) is the preference list submitted by student i, and \(L^k_i \in S\) is the k-th choice in his list. \(\mathbf{L}\) denotes the set of all possible list, and the set of all the probibility disrtibution over \(\mathbf{L}\) is denoted by \(\Delta(\mathbf{L}\)) and \(L_{-i}\) denotes the lists submitted by students other than i.

Assumption 1 Students are expected utility maximizers. and we denote their von Neumann-Morgenstern utilities for each school by a vector \([v_{i,s}]_{s \in S}\).

Assumption 2 The valuations \(v_i \equiv [v_{i,s}]_{s \in S}\) follow a distribution \(F\), which is commonly known by all students.
e.g. suppose there are 3 schools, then \(\mathbf{v}_i = \mathbb{R}^3\), for \(V=\{v_{i,1}\in [0,1],v_{i,2}\in [0,1], v_{i,3}\in [0,1]\}\), we have \(Pr(v_{i} \in V)=\int \mathbb{\Large{1}}\{ v \in V\}\,dF(v)\) to denote the probability or the proportion of students whose valuations are in that set.

Assumption 3 The realization of the valuation \(v_i\) is privately known to student \(i\) himself for each \(i \in I\).

Assumption 4 All schools are acceptable for each students1.

Given submitted \((L_i,L_{-i})\), we use \(a_{s}(L_i,L_{-i}) \in [0,1]\)2 to denote the probability that student \(i\) is admitted by school \(s\) given the above submitted preference profile, and this probability is determined by the mechanism.

Given submitted \((L_i,L_{-i})\), the expected utility of student \(i\) is
\[V_i(v_i,L_i,L_{-i})=\sum_{s \in S}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})v_{i,s}\]

A mixed strategy \(\sigma\) specifies a probability distribution over all lists, and the mixed strategy profile \(\sigma(\mathbf{v}_i): R^{|S|} \rightarrow \Delta(\mathbf{L})\) specifies what mixed strategy \(i\) should apply contingent on his valuation over school is \(\mathbf{v}_i\).

Given applied mixed strategies \((\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})\), the expected utility of student \(i\) is \[V_i(v_i,\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})=\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_i \in \mathbf{L}}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_i\text{is played under $\sigma_i$)}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i})$)}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})v_{i,s}\]

For simplification of the expression, we write \[A_s(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})=\sum_{L_i \in \mathbf{L}}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_i\text{is played under $\sigma_i$)}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\sigma_{-i}$)}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})\] to denote the probability of being admitted to school \(s\) under the mixed strategies.

And we can simplify the notation to be \(V_i(v_i,\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})= A(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})*v_i\), where \(A(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})=[A_s(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})]_{s \in S}\)

School choice as a Bayesian Game

The school choice can be considered as a Bayesian Game consisting of the tuple \((I,\Omega,(\mathbf{L}_i),(\mathbf{v}_i),(\mathbf{F}_{-i}),(\mathbf{V}_i))\), where
\(I\) is the set of students as a finite set of player;
\(\Omega: \omega \times \mathbf{v}_{-i}\) is the state of the realization of the lottery that determines priority and the realization of others' valuations;
\((\mathbf{L}_{i})\) is the set of actions for each student \(i\), which is his submitted list;
\((\mathbf{v}_i)\) is the set of type for each student \(i\), which is his valuations over the schools;
students hold common prior belief on the distribution of other's valuation as \(F_{-i}(\mathbf{v}_{-i})\)
student i's payoff function is given by \(V_i(v_i,L_i,L_{-i})\) above.

Then, a stundent's interim expected payoff (after knowing his valuation \(v_i\)) is \[\begin{aligned}\underset{\text{over }\omega \times \mathbf{v}_{-i}}{E}[V_i(v_i,\sigma(v_i),\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i}))] &=\int V_i(v_i,\sigma_i(v_i),\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i})) \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i})\\&=\int A(\sigma_i,\sigma_{-i})*v_i \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i}) \\&= \int\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_i \in \mathbf{L}}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_i\text{is played under $\sigma_i$)}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\sigma_{-i}$)}a_s(L_i,L_{-i})v_{i,s} \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i}) \end{aligned}\]

Definition 1 A (mixed-strategy) Bayesian Nash Equilibrium is a strategy profile \((\sigma_i(\cdot))_{i \in I}\), such that \[\sigma_i(v_i) = arg\max_{\sigma'_i \in \Delta(\mathbf{L})} \;\;\underset{\text{over }\omega \times \mathbf{v}_{-i}}{E}[V_i(v_i,\sigma(v_i),\sigma_{-i}(v_{-i}))]\] for each \(i\) and \(\forall v_i\).

School choice as a Non-atomic Game

To prove the existence of a symmetric Bayesian Nash Equilibrium of the above game, we can convert it into a nonatomic game, which is a game with complete information and a continuum of non-atomic players. The existence result of non-atomic game is first established by Schmeidler(1973), and then generalized by Khan(1986).

Specially, the school choice game can be considered as a non-atomic game consisting of \(((\mathbf{V},\mathcal{B},\mu),\Delta(\mathbf{L}),\mathbf{X},\mathbf{u})\), where

\((\mathbf{V},\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{V}),\mu)\) is a complete, finite measure space of the types, with \(\mathbf{V}=R^{|S|}\) to be the set of types, \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{V})\) to be the Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra of \(\mathbf{v}\), and \(\mu\) is the non-atomic measure3 on \((\mathbf{v},\mathcal{B}(\mathbf{v}))\), s.t. \(\mu(V)=(|I|-1)\int \mathbb{\Large{1}}\{v \in V\}\,dF(v)\);
\(\Delta(\mathbf{L})\) is the set of mixed strategies for each player;4
\(\mathbf{X}: \mathbf{V} \rightarrow \Delta(\mathbf{L})\) is a measurable map that describes the mixed strategy applied by each type;
\(\mathbf{u}(\cdot,\cdot):\mathbf{V} \times L_1(\mu,X(\cdot)) \rightarrow R^{2^{|S|}}\)5 is the auxiliary utility function, and \[\begin{aligned}u^{j}(v_i,x)&=V(v_i,L_j,\hat{x})\\&=\int A(L_j,\hat{x}(v_{-i}))*v_i \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i}) \\&= \int\sum_{s \in S}\sum_{L_{-i} \in \mathbf{L}^{|I|-1}}\text{Pr(}L_{-i}\text{is played under $\hat{x}(v_{-i}$)}a_s(L_j,L_{-i})v_{i,s} \,dF_{-i}(v_{-i})\\&=\underset{\text{over } \omega \times v_{-j}}E[v_i,L_j,\hat{x}(v_{-i})] \end{aligned}\] is the payoff to player of type \(v_i\) when he submits \(L_j\) while the other player act according to the strategy profile described by \(\hat{x}\), and we construct it to coincide with the interim expected utility of a student whose valuation is \(v_i\), submission is \(L_j\) while others' strategies contingent on their realization of valuations are given by \(x(v_j)\) for \(j \neq i\)

Hence, the payoff for a player of type \(v\) when he applied strategy \(y=(y_1,\cdots,y_{2^{|I|}})\), while others follows strategy x, can be expressed as \(h_{v}(y)=y*u(v,x)\).

Definition 2 An equilibrium of the non-atomic game is the strategy profile \(\hat{x}\), s.t. \[\hat{x}*u(v,\hat{x}) \geq y*u(v,\hat{x}) \;\;\;\; a.e. \forall v\in V\]
