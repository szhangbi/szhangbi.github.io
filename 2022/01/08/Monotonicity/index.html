A student can weakly increase her chance of being assigned to a school by ranking it higher in her list under SOSM and BM.

Posted on 2022-01-08 In Proofs Views: Disqus:

In this post, I provide my (a bit verbose) proof of a monotonicity property shared by both Student-proposing Deferred Acceptance and Immediate Acceptance mechanisms. That is, a student can weakly increase her chance of being assigned to a school by ranking it higher in her list.

Notations

\(I\) for the set of students and \(i\) for a general student, the Ranked Order List submitted by student \(i\) is denoted as \(L_i\), the lists submitted by others are denoted as \(L_{-i}\); \(S\) for the set of schools and \(s\) for a general school, \(q_s\) for the capacity of school \(s\), the priority list over students at school \(s\) is denoted as \(P_i\); SOSM for Student optimal stable mechanism and BM for Boston Mechanism, \(\mu^{M}(i,L_i,L_{-i})\) to be the assignment of student i under mechanism M; we say a student \(i\) moves up a school \(s\) in her list if she ranks \(s\) higher while keeping the relative ranking among the remaining schools unchanged, that is \(|\{s'|s' L_i s \}|<|\{s'|s' L'_i s \}|\) and \(s' L_i s \Leftrightarrow s' L'_i s\).

Lemma 1

Given \(\forall \; (I,S,\mathbf{q},\mathbf{P},L_{-i})\), if \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L_i,L_{-i})=s\) and if i moves up s in her list to have \(L'_i\), then \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\).

Proof by contradiction:
Suppose \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s' \neq s\).
Suppose i applies to s at step t under \(\mu^{SOSM}(L_i,L_{-i})\) and at step \(t'<t\) under \(\mu^{SOSM}(L'_i,L_{-i})\), we know the steps ahead of \(t'\) are identical under the two cases1, such that the application of other students are the same for \(i' \neq i\).

  1. If \(s L_i s'\), then it implies at some step \(k\) before the algorithm terminates, there is a student \(a\) applying to \(s\) under \(\mu'\), who never applies to \(s\) under \(\mu(L)\), and her priority is higher than \(i\), i.e. \(a P_s i\). This must be caused2 by some other student \(b\), who applied to a's original assignment \(\mu(a,Li,L_{-i})\), at a step \(k' < k\). This must be caused by another student \(c\), who applied to b's original assignment \(\mu(b,Li,L_{-i})\), at a step \(k'' < k'\)...
    Continuing with the above argument, we would reach a step \(j < t'\)3, where a student x's behavior deviates from what she did under \(\mu(L)\), contradicting with the fact that all previous steps before \(t'\) shall be identical;

  2. If \(s' L_i s\), then this would directly violate the strategy-proofness of SOSM.

By above, we know \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\).

Lemma 2

Given \(\forall \; (I,S,\mathbf{q},\mathbf{P},L_{-i})\), if \(\mu^{BM}(i,L_i,L_{-i})=s\) and if i moves up s in her list to have \(L'_i\), then \(\mu^{BM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\).

Proof: Under \(\mu^{BM}(i,L_i,L_{-i})\), when i applies to s at step k, school s must not be full, otherwise i would be rejected and not assigned to s. Then, under \(\mu^{BM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})\), when i applies to s at an earlier step \(t'<t\), s must not be full either, i would be accepted at that step and the assignment would be finalized. So \(\mu^{BM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\) as well.

Lemma 3

\(\exists \; (I,S,\mathbf{q},\mathbf{P},L_{-i})\), s.t. \(\mu^{SOSM}(L_i,L_{-i}) = s' L_i s\), and after she moves up s in her list to have \(L'_i\), \(\mu^{SOSM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\).

Proof:Suppose i has top priority at s and s', while lowest priority at any other school, and all student other than i rank s and s; as the bottom two, and for each other school \(s''\), there are \(q_{s''}\) students putting it as the top choice. Under such extreme case, when i would either be accepted by s or s', depending on which school she applies to first.

Lemma 4

\(\exists \; (I,S,\mathbf{q},\mathbf{P},L_{-i})\), s.t. \(\mu^{BM}(L_i,L_{-i}) = s' L_i s\), and after she moves up s in her list to have \(L'_i\), \(\mu^{BM}(i,L'_i,L_{-i})=s\).

Proof: Same as above.

Here are the implications:

Proposition 1

Under SOSM or BM, with whatever distribution of the priorities and preferences, a student i can weakly increase her chance of being assigned to a school s by moving s up in her list.

Proof: Given any realization of the priorities and preferences, if i is originally assigned to s, by lemma 1 and 2, she is still assigned to s if she moves up s.
According to lemma 3 and 4, there are cases where i is originally not assigned to s4, but can be assigned to s after moving up s.
Whether she can strictly increase her chance depends on whether the above cases have possible possibility.

  1. Or, if i ranks s at the top, there is no previous step;↩

  2. If not, at step \(k\) under \(\mu(L')\), there are \(q_s\) students applying to s, each with priority higher than i at s, had they also applied to s under \(\mu(L)\), \(\mu(i,L)=s\) would violate the stability.↩

  3. Or, j=0, this is also a contradiction;↩

  4. Under SOSM, by strategy-proofness, she must be assigned to a school worse than s, and this is not necessary under BM.↩
